Look Around

Usually, my advice is “don’t look around”. Make progress at your own pace. Do your best.

In this post, written for a very specific audience, I take the opposite stance.

Prologue (edit)

I’m sixteen again. I walk up to school and Ice-G is out in front in his Camero. His blood-shot eyes greet me. “Sup, homie.” I roll into first period at there’s four other mf’ers that made it today. Two of them are planning how they are going to jump Daft Punk during gym class. Sh!t. How the f am I going to get a scholarship to U of I surrounded by this bs. If this mfer teacher doesn’t do his f-ing job and teach some trigonometry, I’m going to be repeating this class as College Algebra and paying for it. :skull: I need to gtfo.

What do you mean I need to give up my friends? No way. They’re my rock.

Introduction

I am starting to think that some students do not understand how their effective rate of work compares to others. Especially in the last period of the day, which is currently around 2pm - so really barely after lunch - I see people not completing work that they are capable of doing.

Work Rate

In the software engineering field (computer science), people talk about the 10x engineer. That is someone who does ten times the amout of work of someone else. Is that even possible? Trust your own experience, and apply it to your own situation.

I can tell you from direct observation that there is absolutely such a thing as a 2x student. That means a student who does twice as much work as the typical student.

One day in class I had a worksheet and a review assignment. I watched people finish the worksheet and then stop. Let’s say you quit 20 minutes before the end of class in a 90 minute block. That’s more than 20% of the class time. If you worked at 100% effort for the first part of class, you’re still at a B- (83% of class worked). Think about that.

Sleep

Is your sleep schedule really so bad that you are unable to function in the early afternoon? That urgently needs to be fixed. Talk to an adult in your life.

Every day you can’t function in a block-schedule class, you lose at least two days of learning. Maybe more, because you probably can’t do the homework afterwards, either. Ask yourself: how am I catching up to the people who were there and did their work?

Reactions to Challenges

What should you do when the teacher asks a question and you don’t know the answer? Maybe you don’t recognize the ways you are trying to escape learning.

  • Leave the paper blank?
  • Play with whiteboard markers?
  • Balance your tippy chair on two legs to keep yourself entertained?
  • Wait for the teacher to restate it? Again?
  • Quietly sit there and hope you do not get called on?

None of those work. Learning is slippery and difficult at the start. You need to find a place you can grab on to a topic, a handhold to begin from.

  • Start figuring out what you don’t understand.
  • Can you give an example of the topic?
  • Can you ask the teacher for an example?
  • Did you do something similar recently? Do you have notes you can look at?
  • Sit quietly and think. Your brain works. Give it time.

Prepare your mind

Don’t just walk into class 20 seconds before the bell rings, waiting to be told what to do. Have a have a notebook, have a pencil. Do you

Manage your own behavior.

This means making choices that will help you be more productive. Younger students, especially, but this applies to everyone.

  • Are you gaming through lunch and then unable to focus?
  • Do you need a higher level of activity than you are getting?
  • Walk (or even run) around the school in your free times. The school has treadmills! Walking a mile takes about 20 minutes. You could easily walk a mile every day during your lunchtime and it would be great for your health.
  • There is a 10 minute passing period, and I see some of the worst behavior from people who are in the room with more than five minutes to spare. Take the long route. Do not get to class so early.
  • Are you sitting with people who are making fun of you? Breaking your pencils? Distracting you? Everyone is quick with the advice to avoid gang-bangers and dealers… but really, it’s a matter of degree. If they’re holding you back, try having an honest discussion with them. “Hey, this class is really hard for me and if you’re messing with me I can’t focus.” If they don’t want to be supportive, the mature approach is to distance yourself. Find new friends who are actually supportive of your success.
  • Don’t be a jerk. If you’re the person who is doing stuff for attention that you know is wrong, stop. If someone asks you to stop, respect them. Most people don’t like the idea of adults controlling their lives… so start taking responsibility for yourself. Practice for a time (very soon!!) in which you will actually be independent.
